Recent Research News:  the need for home practice?

There have been a number of papers over the years that have investigated whether home practice is related to beneficial outcomes including whether there is a dose (ie time spent in formal practice)-response effect. More recent papers have examined whether home practice in MBCT impacts specifically on reducing depression relapses.  

In early 2104, the UK Oxford and Bangor Centres published a joint research project comparing 2 types of MBCT courses – the standard one, and one in which the class and home meditation practices were removed completely.  Their results suggested that the meditation practice made no difference to depression outcomes unless you had a history of childhood adversity. Essentially this said that for those without childhood trauma of any kind, just knowing about mindfulness and self-care was enough to protect against relapse but if there was a history of childhood trauma, then the meditation practice facilitated a fuller effect (Crane et al, Behaviour Research and Therapy, 2014). This has caused much debate as it seems to contradict most meditation teachers’ personal experience and clinical experience.

However, two other recent studies have shed further light (Crane et al, BRAT 2014; Perich et al, BRAT 2013). Crane’s study shows that there is a clear association between the amount of practice done and the benefits people notice. If people meditate less than 3 days per week, no benefits are found on depression outcomes. The Perich study is an Australian one and was looking at MBCT for Bipolar Disorder but again noted that the frequency of manic episodes, anxiety and depression were all improved in those who meditated 3 days a week or more.

Clearly ongoing research is needed to help us understand these findings and how mindfulness courses are working!

 


Alumni Training Intensive 2015

Conveying course themes through embodiment, 

interactive inquiry and didactic teaching


18th-23rd July, 2015 
Sangsurya Retreat, Byron Bay, NSW

This is an invitation to all of you who completed the MTIA Level I Intensive or other equivalent professional teacher training in the MBIs  (perhaps through Openground, CTAD, Monash or the CFM) over the last few years.  We welcome you to five days of exploration, learning and communing with MBI colleagues at the beautiful Sangsurya Retreat Centre in Byron Bay. This is a lovely bush setting with ocean views and very comfortable accommodation and wonderful food. You are welcome to come and immerse yourself in this training whether or not you have begun formally teaching MBSR or MBCT.

As well as exploring and deepening our personal mindfulness practice, the Alumni Intensive will have the focus of exploring the rich dance in the MBI classes of communicating course themes through embodiment, didactic teaching and the inquiry process. We will explore this through teaching and inquiring in small groups, examining and practicing different elements of the programs. All of this will be supported by reflection, connecting to the philosophical and theoretical roots of this work and our practice together.

International networks

MTIA is part of a global network of teacher training organisations which is working towards international agreement on mindfulness teacher training pathways, standards and certification protocols so that the standard of MBI teaching can be fostered and maintained.

In April of 2014, Timothea attended meetings at the Center for Mindfulness, UMass, and in October this year, Maura visited each of the 3 most established mindfulness training centres in the UK  (Bangor, Oxford and Exeter) to foster ongoing relationships, explore their training pathways and collaborate on the model we are developing here in Australia. 

The UK centres have been instrumental in the setting up of this international network of training organisations, framing the questions and considerations, and organising the meetings.  The Directors of MTIA will be attending a meeting in July of 2015 in Chester, UK to further develop the network framework.  We are currently meeting with colleagues who are already running Mindfulness teacher training organisation in Hong Kong and Korea to look at establishing an Asia Pacific Network.
